Introduction

Motivation

For this project, we thought it would be interesting to look at how certain statistics affect the overall success of teams in the NBA. From a time where short shorts and converse were the standard, the game of basketball has evolved tremendously over the past 125 years. It is easy to point out key differences in style of play when comparing an NBA game from the 1940s versus a game today, but what about over the past decades? By analyzing team statistics, we hope to dig deeper into how the game of basketball has changed over the course of the last 20 years. Without further ado, lets get after it.

Key aspects of this project include:

  • Data collection
  • Data reorganization and cleaning
  • Graph and model relationships between sets of data
  • Train our model(s) to predict win percentage
  • Comparing stats from 1990s to current stats

Interpreting Scatter Plot Slopes

For each scatter plot, we have printed out the slopes of the regression lines for the plot below it. These slopes can be interpreted as the increase in win percentage for every increase of 1 for the statistic being plotted. As you can see from the first plot, the 2014-2018 slope for win percentage vs. PPG is 0.013. If we scale up the slope a bit, we can say that for an increase of 5 points per game over a season, a teams win percentage is expected to increase by 0.065 ( 0.013 * 5 ). This 6.5% would equate to an extra 5-6 wins in a regular 82 game season.

You can do similar analysis with the other statistics, but there are a few things to consider when looking at these slopes. First is the frequency of the statistic in the sport. When comparing the slopes for blocks per game and points per game, you have to consider tha fact that a block is much less frequent than a point in basketball. So even though the blocks per game slope is much higher, it is much rarer to get 5 extra blocks per game in a season than it is for 5 extra PPG. The second thing to consider is the correlation. We will talk about this more in the next section, but statistics like blocks and steals and much less correlated to win percentage, so they are often worse at predicting the actual change you would see in win percentage if those statistics were affected.

Conclusion and Insight

Our mission for this project was to determine how different statistics affect NBA team success and how these statistics have changed between the late 90s to current day. We started by looking at the summary statistics for individual NBA statistics to understand what impacts win percentage the most and how closely correlated they are to win percentage. Our original pool of statistics consisted of points per game, assists, rebounds, blocks and steals, however, when looking at correlations between variables, we found that field goal percentage and 3-point percentage were two of the most correlated statistics with win percentage. To analyze how certain statistics have changed over time, we ran t-tests to compare mean and standard deviation of each statistic from each time period.

Looking at individual statistics is helpful for understanding the statistics, but when predicting win percentage, we must use multiple statistics at the same time in order to get a more accurate prediction. Because of this, the next step of our project was to train a linear regression model and use this as a way to predict win percentages. We did this for each time period and saw that both models could usually predict win percentage within about .015 (or 1.5 percent). This is quite good considering that this translates to a prediction within 1-2 games won over a season. Finally, to determine how skilled our models are, we ran 5-fold cross validation scores and found our models to achieve about a 60-65% accuracy rate in predicting win percentage.
